# History of the Conflict in Isger — Extended

## Introduction

The conflict now unfolding in Isger is not a sudden uprising caused by one battle, one law, or one ambitious faction. It is the result of centuries of resentment, neglect, fear, and survival. To understand why the country stands on the edge of rebellion at the beginning of this campaign, it helps to understand how Isger came to be ruled, how its people were repeatedly asked to endure hardship alone, and why so many of them no longer believe the old order deserves unquestioned loyalty.

This summary is written from the perspective of what informed people in Isger, Andoran, or Cheliax might reasonably know at the start of the adventure. It focuses on public history, broad political realities, and the recent events that have made the current crisis impossible to ignore.

## Before Cheliax

Long before Isger existed as a kingdom, province, or vassal state, the land was home to the Isgeri tribes. These peoples lived between the Menador Mountains and the Five Kings Mountains, hunting, fishing, farming, trading, and meeting at shared sites to maintain alliances and settle disputes. They were not united under a single crown, but they were not a people without order. Their customs, rivalries, and gatherings shaped the land for thousands of years.

That changed in 2133 AR, when Taldor's Seventh Army of Exploration marched into the region and conquered it by force. The tribes resisted, but they were overrun. The new province was named Isger after the people Taldor had subdued, preserving the name while stripping away the sovereignty behind it. For nearly two thousand years, Isger remained under Taldan rule.

When Cheliax won its own independence from Taldor in 4081 AR, Isger did not become free. Instead, Cheliax annexed it and made it the first vassal of a growing empire. From that point onward, Isger's place in the world was defined by subordination. It was expected to obey Chelish law, serve Chelish interests, and accept rulers chosen for it rather than by it.

## A Country Meant to Serve, Not Decide

Much of Isger's history under Cheliax can be understood in simple terms: the country was expected to provide value, not exercise self-determination. It offered farmland, timber, mineral wealth, and access to trade along the Conerica River. That made Isger important to rulers and merchants, but not always to the people who actually lived there.

This created a familiar pattern. Elites in the capital and nobles with Chelish ties prospered more easily than those in the countryside. Ruling stewards concerned themselves with revenue, administration, and the protection of major routes, while villages, market towns, and remote communities were often left to solve their own problems. Isgeri learned to lean on kin, temples, militias, and stubborn local solidarity because larger powers were unreliable.

This is one reason many Isgeri speak of their country as though it has never truly been allowed to stand on its own. It is a nation ruled from above but sustained from below. In Elidir and among loyalists, Cheliax is often praised as the guarantor of order. Outside the capital, many people believe that order mostly means somebody else deciding which suffering matters and which can be ignored.

## The Goblinblood Wars

If there is one event that defines modern Isger more than any other, it is the Goblinblood Wars. The conflict began when pressure and violence around the Chitterwood escalated into a much wider catastrophe. Hobgoblin warlords rallied goblin forces into a devastating offensive, and the countryside suffered enormously.

Beginning in 4697 AR, settlements burned, roads became dangerous, farms were abandoned, and thousands died. Entire communities were wiped out or scattered. The scale of the devastation permanently altered the way Isgeri people thought about their rulers, their neighbors, and their future.

For many, the most bitter memory is not simply that the war happened, but that the response came too slowly and too unevenly. Steward Hedvend VI came to embody this failure. Many believed he prioritized the capital, the wealthy, and the major arteries of trade while the countryside bled. As towns fell and morale collapsed, soldiers deserted, resources dwindled, and the crisis spread farther.

In the end, Isger was not saved by its own strength alone. The Goblinblood Wars ended only through an uneasy coalition involving Chelish Hellknights, Andoran's Eagle Knights, and other outside support. They halted the offensive, but the land and the people were changed permanently.

## The Wounds That Never Closed

The end of the Goblinblood Wars did not restore Isger to what it had been before. The country emerged with physical scars, political resentment, and social divisions that still define daily life at the start of the campaign.

Veterans carried permanent injuries home. Orphans filled towns and temples. Some villages vanished from the map entirely. Communities that survived did so by becoming harder, more suspicious, and more self-reliant. In the countryside especially, people learned to value resilience over comfort and practical solidarity over grand promises.

The war also poisoned relations between many humans and goblinoids. In some regions, fear hardened into tradition. In others, time and necessity forced uneasy coexistence. Isger at the beginning of this campaign is not a country that has healed. It is a country still living with the consequences of mass death, displacement, and abandonment.

## Aid, Faith, and Divided Loyalty

After the war, Cheliax and the Church of Asmodeus expanded relief efforts across the country. Priests treated the wounded, institutions cared for orphans, and charitable arms of Chelish religion gained influence in places where government had failed. For many citizens, that aid was real and meaningful. Some people survived because it existed. Some communities remember it with sincere gratitude.

Yet the memory of this period is not simple. To many Isgeri, postwar relief looked less like generosity and more like control arriving after neglect. Assistance often came bound up with obligation, religious influence, or deeper loyalty to Cheliax. Communities that had already been left to suffer now found themselves asked to be thankful to the same larger system that had failed them.

This is one of the central truths of Isger's politics: loyalty to Cheliax is not evenly distributed, and it is not always born from love. Some citizens support Chelish rule because they genuinely believe it preserves order. Some because Chelish institutions helped them at their lowest point. Some because they fear what will happen if the current structure breaks apart. Others despise Cheliax, but still distrust the idea of open war. Between those poles lies a broad middle made up of grief, pragmatism, and divided memory.

## Saringallow and the Memory of Resistance

Saringallow matters to the present conflict for reasons older than the Hellbreakers League. The city has long carried a reputation for distrusting distant authority. During the Goblinblood Wars, it survived better than many surrounding communities and became a refuge for the displaced. That gave it both practical importance and political confidence.

Stories endure that Saringallow's people and allies quietly helped hold together anti-goblin cooperation while more powerful figures claimed the credit. Whether every such story is true hardly matters now. What matters is that the city is widely seen as a place where stubborn people organize, endure, and remember.

## Godsrain, Warshards, and a Region on Edge

For years, dissatisfaction with Chelish rule simmered without fully igniting. Then came the Godsrain and the death of Gorum. Across Golarion, that event shattered assumptions about permanence and power. Warshards fell across the Inner Sea, and with them came an atmosphere of fear, ambition, prophecy, and opportunity.

In Isger, this mattered in two ways. First, it made some people wonder whether the great chains of history could break after all. If even a god could fall, perhaps Cheliax was not eternal. Second, it made every struggle feel more dangerous, because no one wanted a rival power to gain divine fragments while they stood idle.

Battle-hardened people, refugees, clergy, laborers, and opportunists all had reason to believe the world was shifting. The old resentments of Isger did not vanish, but they took on a new urgency. Every siege, raid, and sudden military move now invited a new question: who was trying to secure warshards, and what would they become if they succeeded? Even when ordinary people lacked specifics, many feared Cheliax might pair infernal ambition with fragments of divine power.

## From Isgeri Crisis to Hellfire Crisis

What began in Isger is no longer only Isger's problem. Across the Inner Sea, many people now speak of the widening conflict between Andoran and Cheliax as the Hellfire Crisis. Isger was where the spark landed, but larger forces quickly recognized its significance.

Andoran and Cheliax were already bitter rivals. Andoran had long defined itself in opposition to Chelish infernal rule, while Cheliax regarded republican defiance and anti-tyrannical movements as threats to be contained or crushed. Once the crisis in Breachill turned bloody, it became easy for both sides to see the matter as more than a local disturbance.

Other developments reinforced that feeling. Cheliax completed the Aspodean Wall on its border with Andoran, a visible sign that old tensions were hardening into open confrontation. Fighting and blockades at sea began threatening trade throughout the Inner Sea. Even people far from any battlefield could see that merchants, diplomats, and neighboring nations were being pulled into the same fire.

To Isgeri, this broader crisis cuts both ways. On one hand, it suggests Cheliax can be challenged. On the other, it raises the old fear that Isger will once again become a canvas for proxy struggles between stronger powers.

## The Forces People Know by Reputation

Two military traditions loom especially large in public memory.

The first is the Hellknights. Imposing in dark armor and faceless helms, they are widely recognized across the Inner Sea. They teach that order, discipline, and mercilessness are necessary to force the world into peace. Some Hellknights insist they are not servants of Cheliax, and in theory many orders have historically been independent. In practice, ordinary people often care less about that legal distinction than about what Hellknight armor means when it appears in their town.

The second is the Eagle Knights of Andoran. They began as defenders of Andoran's freedom and grew into diplomats, soldiers, privateers, and covert agents in service of that cause. Their public reputation is very different from that of the Hellknights: they are associated with liberty, intervention against tyrants, and the belief that oppressive rule can and should be resisted.

Older Isgeri remember that these two groups once fought on the same side during the Goblinblood Wars. That shared history made the Eagle Knights' return to Isger easier to justify in the eyes of some observers. It also made their later clash with Hellknights feel like proof that the old political balance had broken apart.

## The Hellbreakers League

Out of this atmosphere, the Hellbreakers League became the clearest face of organized resistance inside Isger. The League was founded by idealists, former soldiers, and others sick of watching common people suffer for the failures of rulers and the ambitions of outsiders. Its purpose was straightforward: free Isger from Chelish domination.

At first, the Hellbreakers were more network than army. They relied on sympathizers, safehouses, covert organization, and the moral force of a cause many people privately understood even if they feared to support it openly. Their growing association with a warshard made them harder for enemies to ignore and easier for both allies and rivals to imagine as a real political force.

## Breachill Under Occupation

The immediate crisis that leads into the campaign begins in Breachill. At Hedvend VI's request, and with the support of Queen Abrogail Thrune II, Hellknights of the Order of the Rack moved into the town to root out treason and reclaim power linked to the Hellbreakers. Their occupation was brutal. Citizens were placed under severe pressure, public executions were used to terrify the population, and the town withered under surveillance and repression.

This mattered because Breachill was not just another town. Its suffering became visible, undeniable, and politically explosive. The Hellknights were not quietly policing dissidents in some forgotten district. They were making an example of a community.

When Breachill cried out for help, Andoran answered first with diplomacy. Retired general Reginald Cormoth traveled with a diplomatic retinue to demand that the oppression stop. The Hellknights answered by capturing and executing him publicly.

That act changed the conflict. It turned repression into scandal, grievance into outrage, and local rebellion into a wider confrontation. News of Cormoth's death spread quickly through taverns, barracks, and travelers' tales in both Isger and Andoran, making the affair feel less like a remote outrage and more like a shared insult demanding response.

## The Battle of Hellknight Hill

Public fury over Cormoth's death drove Andoren intervention. Eagle Knights entered Isger, and the confrontation that followed became the Battle of Hellknight Hill. The Hellbreakers joined them. The battle was fierce and costly, and public retellings of it were made even more shocking by reports that the diabolic dragon Kierotheax appeared over the field in support of Cheliax. In the end the anti-Chelish forces prevailed. Breachill was freed, the occupiers were routed, and surviving loyalists withdrew.

That mattered symbolically as much as militarily. For the first time in years, perhaps in generations, Isgeri dissidents and their allies had proof that Chelish force could be met in the open and beaten. The dragon's presence also convinced many observers that the conflict had already grown beyond an ordinary anti-rebel crackdown. The Hellbreakers were no longer only whispered conspirators. They had become participants in a public victory.

## What War Means to Ordinary People

One reason the present conflict feels so dangerous is that war in Isger is not only a matter of banners, decrees, and commanders. War leaves its mark on everything it touches. It creates soldiers and rebels, but also refugees, mechanics, carpenters, chaplains, smugglers, and orphans. It means broken wagons, patched fortifications, funerals, shortages, suspicious checkpoints, and long arguments about loyalty.

That ordinary pressure matters because it shapes how people respond to politics. A villager who remembers a burned farm, a priest who buried the dead, a craftsperson who kept militia gear in repair, or a child orphaned by earlier fighting does not hear speeches about order and freedom in the same way. Every family in Isger is likely to know the conflict through some practical burden even before open battle reaches them.

This is why the country cannot be neatly divided into rebels and loyalists. Some want freedom at any cost. Some fear another round of devastation more than they hate Cheliax. Some admire the Eagle Knights but distrust foreign intervention. Some despise the Hellknights but still worry that disorder will bring famine, banditry, or revenge killings. The political map is tangled because the lived experience of war is tangled.

## Where the Country Stands Now

At the beginning of the adventure, Isger is still under Chelish rule, but it is no longer quiet. The Hellbreakers have momentum. Saringallow has become a center of resistance. Breachill has shown that revolt can succeed, while the broader Hellfire Crisis suggests the struggle may grow far beyond a single town or even a single country.

Hope is rising, but so is fear. The old order has been bloodied, not broken. The rebels have won a victory, not the war. Across the countryside, people are asking not only whether freedom is possible, but whether they are prepared to pay its cost. That is the true state of affairs at the opening of the campaign: a nation full of old wounds, fresh courage, divided loyalties, and the unmistakable sense that history has begun to move again.
